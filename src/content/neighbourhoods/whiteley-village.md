Whiteley Village is Surrey's best-kept architectural secret. Tucked away in 225 acres of woodland between Hersham and Cobham, it's a complete Arts and Crafts village built in just over a decade, and one of the most remarkable examples of planned community housing in Britain.

## The story

William Whiteley, who built a retail empire in Bayswater in the Victorian era and styled himself the "Universal Provider", left the bulk of his estate to build a model village for retired people of modest means. The project was one of the largest philanthropic building schemes of the Edwardian era.

Eight architects — including Walter Cave and Frank Atkinson — each designed a section of the village around a central octagonal green, connected by roads radiating outwards through the woods. The result is an architectural variety that somehow coheres into a single, unified townscape.

## What to see

The central green is the obvious starting point, with its statue of William Whiteley and the domed Temple of Friendship. Walk the perimeter roads to appreciate the range of architectural styles — half-timbered cottages alongside brick-and-tile Arts and Crafts villas, all united by scale and materials.

The woodland walks on the outer paths of the estate are peaceful and well-maintained.

## Visiting

The village is an active retirement community — please be respectful of residents. There is no public transport to Whiteley Village; access is by car or taxi from Hersham or Walton.

Entry to the grounds is free and visitors are welcome to walk through during daylight hours.
