Next to Walton-on-Thames railway station, on a site now being converted to apartments, there once stood one of the most surreal office buildings in Britain.

Walton Court, the headquarters of **Birds Eye Foods**, completed in 1961, was an architectural landmark that architectural historians placed alongside the best of 1960s modernism. It also housed alligators. And penguins. And flamingos. In the courtyards. On purpose.

The story of Walton Court is the story of a particular moment in British corporate culture — optimistic, experimental, and deeply strange — and it deserves to be told before the last residents move into the apartments and the memory fades entirely.

## The Building

Walton Court was designed by the respected practice **Sir John Burnet, Tait and Partners**, and completed in 1961. It was, from the outset, a building intended to make a statement.

The façade combined **plate glass curtain walling** with distinctive **blue vitreous enamelled panels** — a striking, geometric composition that stood in sharp contrast to the conventional office architecture of the period. A long rectangular pool ran alongside the building, its still surface reflecting the geometric grid of glass and enamel back at itself in a piece of deliberate visual wit.

Architectural historian **Elain Harwood** described it as *"an early example of 1960s op-art"* in physical form — a building that played with perception, pattern, and reflection in the same way that Bridget Riley's canvases, emerging from the same cultural moment, played with the eye.

It was, in short, an extraordinary thing to find next to a Surrey commuter station.

## The Animals

Now we get to the animals.

Birds Eye, seeking to create a working environment that was innovative and impressive to visitors, populated the building's internal courtyards with a collection of **exotic animals**. Not potted plants, not an aquarium. Actual, living, warm-blooded-and-otherwise creatures.

The menagerie included:

- **Flamingos** — standing in the courtyard with their extraordinary architectural stillness, as if they too were part of the op-art aesthetic
- **Penguins** — presumably a nod to the company's frozen food identity, though the logic of keeping Antarctic birds in a Surrey office courtyard requires a certain kind of 1960s corporate confidence
- **Alligators** — the presence of which requires no justification, because there is no justification, and that is precisely the point

Staff working at Walton Court could look out of their office windows and observe alligators going about their business in the courtyard below. This was considered, at the time, a reasonable thing.

The arrangement continued until the animals were eventually — and one imagines with some relief on multiple sides — relocated to **London Zoo**.

## The Frozen Food Brain

Walton Court was not merely eccentric. It was genuinely significant as the operational and intellectual headquarters of the company that defined how Britain ate.

Birds Eye had been established in Britain in 1938 as a subsidiary of the American General Foods Corporation, and by the 1960s it dominated the UK frozen food market. The fish finger — introduced in 1955 — had transformed British family eating. By the time Walton Court opened, the company was processing tens of thousands of tonnes of fish, peas, and vegetables annually.

The Walton headquarters housed the company's research, marketing, and management functions. It was, in a very real sense, the brain of the fish finger industry.

In keeping with its forward-thinking self-image, Walton Court also housed what is believed to have been one of the **earliest canteens in the United Kingdom to serve pre-packaged frozen meals** to its employees. The staff of a frozen food company ate frozen food, served from a canteen that was, in miniature, a prototype for the way Britain would come to eat for the rest of the century. The circularity is satisfying.

## The Spitfire Pilot

The story of Walton Court has one more layer, and it is a sombre one.

Adjacent to the Birds Eye headquarters was a **memorial plaque** to **F/Sgt. Charles Sydney**, a Royal Air Force Spitfire pilot who died in 1940. Sydney's aircraft came down near this site while he was defending the **Brooklands aircraft factories** in Weybridge — one of the most important aircraft production centres in Britain during the Battle of Britain.

Brooklands, just a mile or two from Walton, produced Hurricanes and Wellington bombers throughout the war and was a priority target for the Luftwaffe. Pilots like Sydney flew to defend it. The proximity of his crash site to what became, two decades later, the headquarters of a peacetime consumer goods company captures something of the distance Britain had travelled between 1940 and 1961 — from existential threat to op-art courtyards and penguin colonies.

The memorial plaque stood next to the building for many years. It has since been moved during the site's redevelopment. Its exact current location is not confirmed at time of writing — if you know where it is, please [let us know](/contact/).

## The End

Walton Court's later history is a familiar story. The **Grade II listed building** — a designation that recognised its architectural significance — stood empty for approximately a decade following Birds Eye's departure. Listed status protects a building from casual demolition, but not from slow decline or, ultimately, from a sufficiently determined developer with a sufficiently compelling planning application.

The building has now been demolished and the site is being redeveloped as residential apartments.

The pool is gone. The blue vitreous panels are gone. The alligators have been at London Zoo for fifty years. The fish fingers are still in every supermarket in Britain.

## Finding Walton Court Today

The site is immediately adjacent to **Walton-on-Thames railway station** — the development currently underway there is where Walton Court stood. There is nothing left to see of the original building, but the location itself is worth knowing: the next time you wait on the platform, you are standing within a few hundred metres of where, once, a company built a glass-and-enamel palace for the frozen food age, stocked the courtyards with exotic animals, and employed a canteen that fed the future.

It is, by any measure, a more interesting history than most office blocks can claim.
