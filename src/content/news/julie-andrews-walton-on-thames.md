On the first of October 1935, a girl named Julia Elizabeth Wells was born in Walton-on-Thames, Surrey. The world would come to know her as Julie Andrews — the actress and singer whose voice, warmth, and extraordinary range made her one of the defining performers of the 20th century.

It's a connection Walton doesn't shout about enough.

## Early life in Surrey

Julie's mother, Barbara Wells, was a pianist, and her father, Edward Wells, a teacher. The family lived in Walton-on-Thames in the years immediately after Julie's birth, before moving to Hersham and later to Beckenham as her parents' marriage broke down.

Her mother remarried a Canadian vaudevillian performer named Ted Andrews — a talented singer who recognised something extraordinary in his stepdaughter's voice. By the age of eight, Julie had been discovered to possess a remarkable four-octave coloratura soprano range. Ted began including her in his touring stage act, and a career was born.

The Surrey years were foundational. It was here, in these quiet commuter-belt streets not far from the Thames, that one of the greatest voices in entertainment history first drew breath.

## Stage and screen

Julie Andrews made her professional stage debut in the West End pantomime *Cinderella* in 1947 — she was just twelve years old. By her early twenties she was a Broadway sensation, originating the role of Eliza Doolittle in *My Fair Lady* (1956) and Guinevere in *Camelot* (1960).

Hollywood came calling with *Mary Poppins* (1964), for which she won the Academy Award for Best Actress — a remarkable debut in a film career that would span decades. The following year, *The Sound of Music* (1965) cemented her status as a global icon. Her performance as Maria von Trapp, twirling on an Austrian hillside, became one of cinema's most enduring images.

Later films — *Victor/Victoria* (1982), *The Princess Diaries* (2001), *Enchanted* (2022) — showed the range and longevity of a performer who never seemed to age out of relevance.

## A voice silenced, then returned

In 1997, Julie underwent throat surgery that damaged her vocal cords, robbing her of the singing voice that had defined her career. It was a devastating loss, both personally and for audiences worldwide.

With extraordinary resilience, she redirected her energies into writing — authoring a series of beloved children's books under her married name, Julie Edwards — and into directing and producing. In later years, some degree of her voice returned, and she has spoken movingly about learning to find new purpose after loss.

## Her legacy

Dame Julie Andrews was made a Dame Commander of the Order of the British Empire in 2000. She holds an Honorary Academy Award for lifetime achievement and has received virtually every major entertainment accolade there is to receive.

For Walton-on-Thames, the connection is a quiet source of pride. There is no monument, no heritage plaque, no tourist trail. Just the knowledge that on an October morning in 1935, in a Surrey town on the banks of the Thames, something rather remarkable began.

## Visiting Walton-on-Thames

If you're visiting with Julie Andrews in mind, the town itself is the destination — the streets she grew up near, the Surrey landscape she knew as a child. Hersham, where the family also lived, is a short walk or bus ride from Walton town centre, centred on its lovely village green.

The wider Surrey Hills, visible from higher ground around the town, provided the kind of English pastoral backdrop that runs through so much of her public persona — even when she was singing in Austrian meadows.

*"I have a slight Dick Van Dyke problem. I have a tendency to go over the top."* — Julie Andrews, on her own perfectionism. A very Walton-on-Thames kind of understatement.
