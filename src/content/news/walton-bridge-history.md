There has been a crossing of the Thames at Walton for over 270 years, and its history is more extraordinary than most people know.

## The Canaletto Bridge

In 1748, a remarkable timber bridge was completed at Walton — a Chinese-inspired structure designed by James Paine, with two overlapping arches creating an unusual double-diamond profile. It was considered one of the engineering wonders of its age.

In 1754, the Venetian artist Giovanni Antonio Canal, known as Canaletto, painted the bridge. It is one of only two paintings he made of English subjects during his extended stay in London. The painting now resides in the Yale Center for British Art in New Haven, Connecticut — rather a long journey for a view of the Surrey bank.

The timber bridge didn't last — it was replaced in 1788 — but Canaletto's canvas preserved its image for posterity.

## Later crossings

A succession of bridges followed across the 18th and 19th centuries, each replaced as it deteriorated or became too narrow for growing traffic. By the 20th century, the crossing at Walton was a busy commuter and freight route, and the 1953 concrete structure that served for decades eventually reached the end of its life.

## The current bridge

The current Walton Bridge opened in September 2013 after several years of construction. It's a graceful cable-stayed structure — a single tower and radiating stay cables giving it a distinctive profile on the Surrey skyline. It carries two lanes of road traffic in each direction and has a separate pedestrian and cycle path.

The opening of the new bridge was a significant moment for local residents, many of whom had endured years of traffic disruption during construction. From the riverside path below, it's a genuinely handsome piece of infrastructure.

If you want to see the bridge at its best, walk along Cowey Sale in the late afternoon when the setting sun catches the cables and tower.
