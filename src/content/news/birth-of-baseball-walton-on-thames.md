When people think of baseball, they think of America — Yankee Stadium, the World Series, hot dogs, and the crack of a wooden bat on a summer afternoon. What they rarely think of is a Surrey field in the rain, a future heir to the throne, and an Edwardian cricket ground with a blue plaque on the wall.

They should.

Walton-on-Thames holds a unique and largely unsung place in global sporting history: it is the site of the first recorded game of baseball ever played, 26 years before the United States of America existed.

## The Match of September 1749

On a Tuesday in September 1749, on the grounds of the **Ashley Park estate** in Walton-on-Thames, a group of aristocrats gathered to play a game they called "Bass-Ball."

The participants were not ordinary sportsmen. The game was played by **Frederick, Prince of Wales** — son of King George II and father of the man who would eventually lose the American colonies — and his close companion **Charles Sackville, the Earl of Middlesex**, whose wife owned the Ashley Park estate at the time.

The weather, by all accounts, was atrocious. The Whitehall Evening Post of 19 September 1749 reported that the Prince and Lord Middlesex had "played at Bass-Ball, at Walton in Surry" the previous Tuesday, despite "extreme bad" weather conditions. Undeterred, the royal party and their associates reportedly kept going for several hours.

That newspaper report — a single line buried in an 18th-century London paper — would remain hidden in the archives for over two and a half centuries.

## The Discovery

The Walton match was unknown to sporting historians until **David Block**, a dedicated baseball researcher and author of *Baseball Before We Knew It*, uncovered the reference while trawling through historical newspaper archives. His discovery, announced in 2013, immediately rewrote the sport's history.

Before Block's find, the earliest known written reference to baseball being *played* was a 1755 diary entry describing a game in **Guildford** — itself remarkable for being in Surrey rather than America. The Walton match pushed the record back a further six years, and moved the geographic heart of baseball's origins firmly into the Surrey commuter belt.

The sport had been *mentioned* in print even earlier — in the 1744 children's book *A Little Pretty Pocket-book*, which described "Base-Ball" alongside other games — but the 1749 Walton match is the first recorded instance of an actual game being played, by named players, at a specific location.

## A Different Game

The baseball played at Ashley Park in 1749 was not the game played today at Wrigley Field or Dodger Stadium. The equipment and rules differed considerably from the modern professional sport.

According to Block's research, the ball used in 18th-century baseball was **considerably softer** than modern baseballs. More strikingly, there was **no bat**: the batter struck the ball with the **palm of their hand**, not a wooden implement. The game was, in essence, a form of what we might now call rounders — the English game that many historians believe formed one of baseball's direct predecessors.

This distinction matters. It does not diminish Walton's claim — the Whitehall Evening Post clearly names the game "Bass-Ball," not rounders — but it contextualises baseball as a sport that evolved over centuries rather than one invented whole-cloth in an American field.

## The Blue Plaque

On **7 July 2019**, almost exactly 270 years after that rain-soaked royal match, a **blue heritage plaque** was unveiled at **Walton Cricket Club on Ashley Road** — on or near the very ground where the 1749 game was played.

The ceremony was organised by the **British Plaque Trust** and attracted considerable media attention. The unveiling was performed in the company of comedian and Walton local **Bobby Davro** and double Olympian **Bill Nankeville**, with representatives from the world of both British and American baseball in attendance.

The plaque reads simply but significantly: marking Ashley Park as the site of the first recorded game of baseball, 1749.

BBC News covered the unveiling, as did outlets across the United States — where the story generated equal parts fascination and gentle discomfort about the sport's decidedly un-American origins.

## Ashley Park Today

The Ashley Park estate no longer exists as a private country house. The grounds were developed over the course of the 20th century, and the area is now a residential neighbourhood in the west of Walton-on-Thames. The cricket club on Ashley Road occupies a portion of what was once the estate's grounds.

The blue plaque is visible at the cricket club, and the ground itself — a proper English cricket ground with a pavilion and manicured outfield — has an atmosphere that makes the historical connection feel entirely plausible. This is the kind of ground where gentlemen in the 18th century would absolutely have played bat-and-ball games on a Tuesday afternoon, weather permitting.

Or, as the historical record confirms, even when it wasn't.

## What It Means

Walton-on-Thames has an unusual concentration of historical distinctions for a Surrey commuter town. Canaletto painted the bridge. Julie Andrews was born here. The Romans may have crossed the Thames at Cowey Stakes. And in 1749, on the grounds of a private estate, a Prince and an Earl played the first recorded game of a sport that would go on to define an entire national culture on the other side of the Atlantic.

None of this is trumpeted loudly enough. There is no Baseball Hall of Fame exhibit about Walton, no tourist infrastructure built around the connection. Just a cricket club, a blue plaque on a wall, and the extraordinary fact itself — quietly waiting to be discovered by the people who live here, and by visitors who come looking.

It is, in its understated Surrey way, rather magnificent.
