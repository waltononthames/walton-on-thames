[Walton-on-Thames](/neighbourhoods/walton-on-thames/) has produced or hosted a genuinely varied list of notable people across three centuries, from an eighteenth-century admiral to a punk-adjacent singer-songwriter. What follows is ordered roughly by how widely each name is recognised today, starting with the person Walton is most likely to be known for.

<!-- IMAGE: rodney-house-plaque.jpg — alt: "Commemorative plaque near the site of Rodney House, Julie Andrews's birthplace" -->

## Julie Andrews

Dame Julie Andrews, actress, singer and author, was born Julia Elizabeth Wells at Rodney House maternity home on Rodney Road, Walton-on-Thames, on 1 October 1935. Her mother, Barbara Morris Wells, was a piano teacher, and her father, Ted Wells, taught woodwork; after her parents separated, her mother remarried the tenor Ted Andrews, and Julie grew up performing alongside her stepfather from a young age. She became the youngest solo performer to appear at a Royal Variety Performance in 1948, aged twelve, and went on to a career spanning stage, film and television across more than seven decades. Walton's connection to Andrews is one of the town's most consistently cited claims to fame, and the site of her birth is still marked locally.

## Madeleine Albright

The future United States Secretary of State lived in Walton-on-Thames as a child during the Second World War, part of the wave of European families who found wartime refuge in the area. Albright went on to become, in 1997, the first woman to serve as US Secretary of State, and her early years in Walton form a small but genuine footnote to a career built almost entirely on the other side of the Atlantic.

## Admiral George Brydges Rodney

Admiral George Brydges Rodney was born in 1718 and went on to become one of the most significant Royal Navy commanders of the eighteenth century, best known for his decisive victory at the Battle of the Saintes in 1782 during the American Revolutionary War. His connection to Walton reaches into the present day indirectly: the eighteenth-century bridge scheme associated with the town's crossing history involved figures from the same circle of Georgian naval and political society that Rodney belonged to, and his name persists locally, including in Rodney Road, close to Julie Andrews's birthplace.

## Nick Lowe

Nick Lowe, the singer-songwriter and producer best known for "Cruel to Be Kind" and for producing Elvis Costello's early albums, was born in Walton-on-Thames in 1949. His career has run from the pub rock scene of the 1970s through a long and well-regarded solo output, and he remains one of the more critically respected British songwriters of his generation.

## Natascha McElhone

The actress Natascha McElhone was born in Walton-on-Thames on 14 December 1971. She has built a long screen career in both British and American film and television, including roles in *The Truman Show* and the television series *Californication*, and remains one of the more prominent contemporary names with a genuine Walton birthplace.

## Ian Rank-Broadley

Ian Rank-Broadley, born in 1952, is a sculptor whose most widely seen work is arguably also his least individually recognised: he designed the fourth definitive coinage portrait of Queen Elizabeth II, used on British coins from 1998 onwards, meaning his work has passed through more hands than almost any other living British sculptor's.

## Matthew Brittin

Matthew Brittin, born in 1968, has held senior leadership roles at Google, including as head of Google's operations across Europe, the Middle East and Africa, representing Walton's more recent, corporate connection to the wider world.

## Samuel Croxall

Samuel Croxall, an eighteenth-century clergyman and writer, is remembered chiefly for his widely read English edition of *Aesop's Fables*, first published in the early eighteenth century and reprinted for generations afterwards, making him one of Walton's older literary connections.

## Susan Ertz

Susan Ertz, a novelist active through the middle of the twentieth century, wrote a substantial body of popular fiction and is remembered as one of the more commercially successful British novelists of her era, with a biographical connection to the Walton area.

## Bede Griffiths

Bede Griffiths, the Benedictine monk and interfaith theologian known for his work bridging Christian monasticism and Hindu spiritual tradition in India, had roots connecting him to the Walton area earlier in his life, before the decades in India that made his later reputation.

## Jerome Kern

The American composer Jerome Kern, later responsible for *Show Boat* and standards including "Ol' Man River", is a Walton resident only briefly and only by marriage. He met Eva Leale, daughter of the landlord of the Swan Inn, during a Thames boat trip in 1909, and the couple married at [St Mary's Church](/history/st-marys-church/) on 25 October 1910, before Kern returned to the United States to build the career that would make him one of the most significant figures in American musical theatre. It is a brief Walton chapter in a much longer American story, but a well-documented one, marked today by a plaque on the wall of the Swan.

## A cultural cross-section

Read together, this list says something about Walton that no single entry can: a town small enough to have a single high street has still produced a Hollywood-scale actress, a Cabinet-level American diplomat, a Georgian admiral, a working songwriter, and the man who designed the portrait on the nation's coins. Much of that concentration traces back, directly or indirectly, to the same period that made Walton [one of Britain's earliest film production centres](/history/film-studios/): a town close enough to London to attract talent, but distinct enough to give that talent room to work.
